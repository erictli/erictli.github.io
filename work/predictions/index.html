Uber Eats • Case Study

Improving the Uber Eats restaurant tablet to make couriers wait less

The opportunity

On average, our couriers wait at restaurants for over 5 minutes per order. This wait time is expensive, adding up to hundreds of millions of dollars per year. Reducing average wait times by just 1 second could save millions of dollars.

Starting in the field

Today, we have a machine-learning model that uses dozens of signals to predict when to send couriers to pickup food. When we're wrong, couriers and food end up waiting. The main way that restaurants can interact with our predictions is through our restaurant dashboard tablet. We tell restaurants when food is due and allow restaurants to delay orders through our help panel.

In order to understand the causes of wait time, we conducted field research with restaurants in Chicago and London. Through research, we wanted to understand how restaurants were interacting with our predictions and tablet features. We also wanted to better understand what factors caused variation in restaurant prep times and whether these were captured in our models. I worked with our researcher, product manager, and data scientists to define research objectives, write a research script, and conduct interviews.

The times we show on our order cards confused users.

Our tablet is often one of many things that require attention.

Through our research, we identified three key opportunities:

  • Restaurants have low awareness of when couriers will arrive because the way we display times isn't clear → How might we improve pickup time comprehension by redesigning our order cards?
  • Some restaurants didn't know that they could delay orders while others overused delays because they weren't aware of the consequences → How might we make delays discoverable while discouraging overuse?
  • "Busyness" is the main cause of delays but is hard for us to predict or detect (e.g. a large dine-in group, orders on competitor apps) → How might we enable restaurants to provide us with real-time busyness signals?

More informative order cards

In order to adhere to pickup times, restaurants needed to be aware of them. Thus, it was very important that we improved the clarity and consistency of the times that we showed restaurants throughout the experience.

During our field research, we started putting concepts in front of users. We showed users multiple order cards variations and found that all restaurants preferred countdown times over clock times and better understood cards with labels and integrated courier information.

The time display variations we tested with users. All users preferred the last version.

From there, I broadly explored concepts for re-imaging our order and courier card layout. Through reviews with product, engineering, and other designers, we refined our concepts down to a final version that solved the problems we discovered in research:

  • We previously messaged both static "due times" as well as real-time courier ETAs. We decided to switch to a single system of always showing our best guess of the pick up ETA.
  • We improved the clarity of pickup times by moving from clock to countdown times and adding labels and visual states that communicated the urgency and status of orders at a glance. We also reduced rendundancy and clutter in the UI by integrating our courier and order cards.
  • We added new statuses (e.g. courier waiting) and removed statuses that were confusing (e.g. courier late).

In addition to making changes that supported our goal of reducing courier wait times, we also made a number of improvements to the platform and design system. We made improvements to the way order types were labeled (e.g. pickup, delivery, bring your own courier, catering) and also improved labeling of batched orders. I also collaborated with our restaurant platform designers to get buy in on improvements to our design system that aligned our dated restaurant dashboard with the broader Uber design system.

Improvements to order delays

In addition to improving pickup time clarity, we also wanted to improve the discoverability of our order delay feature while improving the content and UX to encourage responsible delays.

Previously, our order delays were hidden in the help menu. It was also easy for restaurants to delay orders for longer than they needed.

Previously, we had hidden the feature to prevent abuse but this had unintended consequences. So, we decided instead to add an appropriate amount of friction and feedback to delays to make sure they were intentional. We added an entrypoint to the header of our order details modal and updated our order delay UX to use a stepper pattern. We also reframed our time messaging to focus on total prep time instead of additive time. Finally, we added dynamic messaging that increased in severity as the user delayed for longer.

Accounting for unpredicted busyness

Finally, to account for unpredicted delays, we also introduced a feature that allows restaurants to indicate how busy they are. Whereas delays only affect individual orders that have already been placed, busy mode allows restaurants to proactively add time to all orders. This allows restaurants to set expectations with customers before the order is placed and also allows restaurants to dampen demand without having to completely pause their tablet.

Next steps

At the moment these features are in development and we're preparing for launch. We'll want to evaluate how the features impact marketplace metrics including wait time, average delivery times, and restaurant and courier feedback.

We also see further opportunities to better handle batched orders, encourage responsible usage of the ready button, communicate order status to couriers, improve reporting and analytics for operational metrics, and make additional changes to our tablet and receipts to improve workflow efficiency.
