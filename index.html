Bread checkout optimization

Analytics • User Research • Product Design

Bread’s checkout experience is one of the most important areas of our product. It is the first interaction most consumers have with Bread and any increase (or decrease) to our conversion rate can meaningfully impact our revenue and the revenue of our retail partners.

When I joined Bread, one of my first priorities was to set up actionable tagging of our checkout funnel. We mapped out the flows we wanted to measure and set up reporting. We also ran surveys, usability tests, and user interviews to better understand the “jobs” that shoppers were hiring “Bread” for and to immerse ourselves in the context of shoppers who were considering financing. Through this research, we gained a better understanding of why customers were choosing financing, what factors were important to them, and what areas of the user experience shoppers were adding friction to the funnel.

Alongside this research, we began making improvements to our checkout flow. We designed and launched our first A/B test of a new experience for presenting loan options to consumers. Back then, our traffic was relatively low and the test took a couple of months to reach significance, but ultimately led to a 5% improvement in conversion. Since then we've continued to improving our checkout experience, including running succesful tests on our prequalification form and launching new checkout products focused on specific areas of the purchase funnel.

Bread payment experience

User Research • Product Design

Bread's loan repayment experience is a very important for both Bread and the consumers who take out loans with us. Creating a great user experience helps to ensure that Bread gets paid, our operations team isn't flooded with questions, and consumers are able to easily navigate the stressful event of giving away their hard-earned money. The first project I worked on at Bread was optimizing the loan repayment experience. The UX was confusing, payments were being misapplied, and many customers were calling our support line with questions.

We approached this problem by first trying to understand our users and how they thought about making payments. We developed and conducted user surveys and interviews to help us understand the motivations and context around how our users thought about making payments. After categorizing the types of payments users wanted to make, we designed a new experience, created high fidelity prototypes in Invision and Framer and tested them with users before launching an updated payment flow. With the new flow, we saw a reduction in payment mistakes and support calls about payments. We also introduced a more customer-friendly way for borrowers to make principal prepayments (and reduce their interest cost), which now accounts for a significant percentage of all payments.

Capture.com product

Product Design

Capture helps brands and publishers find, display, and license real-time social content. Our customers included CNN, Buzzfeed, The Washington Post, and The Weather Channel. During my time at Capture, we redesigned our core product (which allowed customers to search and acquire rights for social photos and videos in real-time), launched a self-service sign-up and payments flow, and built new features for e-commerce and brand marketing customers. We also concepted, designed, and pitched an on-demand video product that made it easy for publishers to get on-demand video coverage of breaking news events. As the only designer, I also helped create prototypes and presentations for partnership discussions, which ultimately resulted in Capture being acquired.

Topology

Fashion Design • Product Design • Brand Design • Marketing • Operations

Along with a friend, I launched Topology, a direct-to-consumer brand that makes button-down shirts in the USA using unique Japanese fabrics. With no experience in fashion design or manufacturing, we sourced fabrics, found a manufacturer, and created 20 prototype shirts. After selling those in a of couple weeks, we decided to launch a full collection. We conducted customer research, figured out our supply chain, built an e-commerce site, took product and lifestyle photos, and launched and marketed the brand. A few hundred shirts later, we continue to operate Topology, you can visit us here.
